# Cross-paper synthesis guidelines

## Start from the question

Define the synthesis dimensions before grouping results:

- population, material, corpus, or setting;
- intervention, exposure, input, predictor, or phenomenon;
- comparator or context;
- outcome, construct, theme, or performance target;
- timeframe;
- design and unit of analysis.

## Preserve evidence levels

- Count sources, studies, datasets, and claims separately.
- Prefer primary sources for primary results.
- Use reviews for coverage and synthesis claims, not as duplicate primary evidence.
- Keep empirical results, author interpretations, reviewer inferences, and recommendations separate.
- Treat retractions, corrections, protocols, supplements, and duplicate reports explicitly.

## Integrate without overclaiming

Assess:

1. directness to the question;
2. design appropriateness;
3. risk of bias or quality concerns;
4. effect direction and magnitude;
5. precision or evidentiary richness;
6. consistency and meaningful heterogeneity;
7. transferability and boundary conditions;
8. publication and availability bias.

Do not use p-value vote counting. Do not pool studies merely because they report the same outcome label. If quantitative pooling is justified, document the effect measure, dependency handling, model, heterogeneity, and sensitivity analyses.

## Design-aware synthesis

### Quantitative empirical studies

Compare estimands, populations, units, follow-up, adjustment sets, and uncertainty. Distinguish absence of evidence from evidence of little or no effect.

### Qualitative studies

Synthesize themes with context, convergence, divergence, negative cases, and reflexive limits. Frequency is not automatically importance.

### Computational studies

Compare models only under compatible datasets, splits, metrics, and evaluation settings. Separate benchmark performance from external validity and deployment value.

### Reviews and meta-analyses

Map overlapping primary studies before combining conclusions. Consider search date and methodological quality.

### Conceptual or theoretical literature

Map definitions, premises, argument structure, predictions, contradictions, and testable implications. Do not express propositions as effect estimates.

### Policy, guideline, or commentary literature

Separate empirical basis, value judgment, feasibility assumption, stakeholder perspective, and recommendation strength.

## Recommended report structure

1. Executive conclusion
2. Scope, corpus origin, and exclusions
3. Source and study-design map
4. Verified findings by question
5. Conflicts and heterogeneity
6. Design-aware quality appraisal
7. Integrated interpretation
8. Evidence gaps and unavailable full texts
9. Recommendations with strength and conditions
10. Claim-to-source audit appendix

## Evidence-bound wording

Use:

> Under conditions Y, Study A supports X. Study B is directionally consistent but differs in population, design, or measurement. Together they support hypothesis H with stated limitations.

Avoid:

> The literature proves X.

Label statements as:

- **Source fact**: directly verified bibliographic or methodological information.
- **Evidence**: claim verified against the full text.
- **Inference**: cross-source integration.
- **Recommendation**: proposed action.
- **Risk/uncertainty**: bias, conflict, missing evidence, or transferability limit.
